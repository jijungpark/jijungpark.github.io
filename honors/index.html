Psychology Honors Thesis

"The Relationship between Set-shifting and Weight among Adolescents"

Click to enlarge the poster
×

Introduction

The prevalence of overweight and obesity is rising at an alarming rate worldwide, presenting a serious health epidemic. Previous studies have demonstrated that cognition is negatively impacted by obesity, and thus excess body weight is a risk factor for not only physical health problems, but also cognitive impairment. Set-shifting is a key component of executive functions defined as the cognitive flexibility to adapt behaviors in response to changing environment. This study aims to investigate the relationship between set-shifting and weight among adolescents between 16 and 22 years of age. It is hypothesized that set-shifting abilities are better in adolescents at healthy weights and decline as their weight increases.

Method

Participants
Participants in this study included a total of 160 adolescents and young adults between 16 and 22 years of age. Of those participants, 66 were male and 94 female. The participants were divided into two weight status categories: obese and healthy weight. In total, there were 108 obese (BMI >= 30 kg/m^2 or 95th percentile) and 52 healthy weight participants (BMI < 30 kg/m^2 or < 95th percentile). The two study groups were matched demographically on age, gender, education, and race.
Design & Procedure
Participants who met inclusion criteria were administered a comprehensive battery of neuropsychological assessments on two separate days to examine the primary cognitive domains most associated with weight. The focus for this study was executive functioning, specifically set-shifting as measured by the following objective tests: Trail Making Test, Stroop Test, and Category Test. The research design of this study was correlational and non-experimental.

Results

Demographics
In terms of demographics, there were no differences between healthy weight and obese groups on age, sex, education, ethnicity, and estimated full-scale IQ. As expected, BMI differed significantly between healthy weight (M = 21.5775, SD = 1.81487) and obese (M = 35.2623, SD = 4.73870) groups, t(158) = -20.100, p < .001. Hence, the results of the two-tailed independent samples t-test revealed that demographic variables were not confounded with weight and that they were not contributing to the potential differences between the two weight groups in set-shifting.
Cognitive Data
Results indicated that there was a significant difference between healthy weight and obese groups on Trails Making difference score (B-A), with a p-value of .027. However, no significant weight group differences were found for the variables on the other two tests.

Discussion

Although the study only found a significant weight group difference in Trails B-A, it is more diagnostic than the other two tests in predicting set-shifting ability due to removing the influence of motor speed and visual scanning speed on performance.
Strengths
  1. Large sample size
  2. Demographically well-matched groups
  3. Standardized neuropsychological measures
Limitation
  1. Cross-sectional design - does not take into account individual variability in rates of maturation of the frontal lobe during adolescence
  2. Unknown obesity trajectories - some participants could have been obese for a long periods of time while others could have only recently become obese and thus, any possible differences in set-shifting ability may not have appeared yet
  3. Young and healthy participants - they may still have the capability to perform well on set-shifting tasks
